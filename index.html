SWOT & Technology By Yunis Kazimli

What are the benefits and risks of constantly improving technology?

Technology is constantly changing, and in many ways that is a good thing. New tools, smarter systems, and better devices make daily life easier and more efficient. We can communicate faster, study online, automate work, and solve problems that were much harder in the past. In business, for example, modern technology helps companies save time, reduce mistakes, and make better decisions through data. For these reasons, I believe technological progress is necessary and mostly positive.

However, every improvement also brings risks. A simple SWOT analysis shows this clearly. The strengths of evolving technology are speed, efficiency, innovation, and better access to information. The weaknesses include dependence on devices, reduced face-to-face communication, and the fact that not everyone can keep up with rapid change. The opportunities are very exciting: smarter healthcare, better education, cleaner industry, and more flexible work. On the other hand, the threats are serious, especially cybercrime, privacy issues, job loss through automation, and the spread of misinformation.

For me, the most important part of this SWOT analysis is the threats. Technology develops faster than many people can understand or control. This means that systems become more powerful, but also more vulnerable. A security problem in one device or platform can affect thousands or even millions of users. The same innovation that improves life can also create dependence and new dangers.

Overall, I think technology should continue to improve, but not without responsibility. Progress is valuable only when it is combined with security, ethics, and human awareness. In my opinion, the goal should not simply be more technology, but better technology that people can actually trust.

Devices & Vulnerabilities By Ishak Gairaa

My opinion on today’s technologies and their vulnerabilities

Today’s technologies are everywhere. Most people use their phones and laptops from morning until night, often without even thinking about how much personal information they store on them. I think modern technology is extremely useful, and I would not want to live without it. It helps me study, communicate, organize my work, and access information in seconds. At the same time, I am aware that these devices also have vulnerabilities, and I try not to ignore them.

In my opinion, one of the biggest problems is that people become too comfortable with technology. We use the same apps every day, so they start to feel harmless. But in reality, our devices can be exposed to data leaks, phishing attacks, malware, weak passwords, or unsafe networks. Even something simple like clicking on the wrong link or downloading the wrong file can create problems. That is why I think it is important to be careful, even during normal daily use.

I would not say that I am paranoid, but I do pay attention. I try to update my software regularly, use stronger passwords, and avoid suspicious websites or public Wi-Fi when possible. I know that no device is ever completely safe, but basic habits can reduce many risks. For me, awareness is already an important form of protection.

So, I do not ignore technological vulnerabilities. I accept that they are part of using modern devices. Technology gives us convenience, speed, and many opportunities, but it also requires responsibility. In the end, I think the best attitude is to enjoy the advantages of technology while staying realistic about its weaknesses.

This section includes two short writing tasks by Ishak Gairaa. The first text reflects on the TED Talk about AI and learning. The second text discusses the future of authentication methods by 2040.

TED Talk Reflection By Ishak Gairaa

Is AI Making Us Dumber? My Opinion

After watching Charlie Gedeon’s TEDx talk, I do not think the main problem is that AI automatically makes people dumber. For me, the bigger problem is how people use it. AI can be a very useful tool, but it can also make students avoid the hard parts of learning if they are not careful.

One point I found interesting is the idea of cognitive offloading. This means that we give some of our thinking to a tool instead of doing it ourselves. I think this happens often with AI. For example, if a student asks AI to explain a topic after trying to understand it first, that can be helpful. But if the student simply asks AI for the final answer and copies it, then the learning process becomes weaker.

I also agree with the speaker that education often rewards results more than learning. Many students focus on grades, deadlines, and passing exams. In that situation, AI becomes very tempting because it gives fast answers. The problem is that fast answers do not always mean real understanding.

In my opinion, AI should be used more like a training partner than a replacement for thinking. It can ask questions, give examples, check grammar, or explain difficult ideas. However, students should still verify information, compare sources, and develop their own arguments.

So, I would not say AI is making us dumber by itself. It depends on our habits. If we use AI passively, we may lose skills. If we use it actively and critically, it can actually help us learn better.

Digital Identity Essay By Ishak Gairaa

What Will Replace Today’s Authentication Methods by 2040?

By 2040, I think today’s authentication methods will not be replaced by one single technology, but by a combination of several methods working together in the background. Passwords will probably become much less important, because they are easy to forget, reuse, steal, or attack through phishing. Even today, many people are already tired of managing too many passwords.

In my opinion, the future will be based on continuous and multi-factor authentication. This means that a system will not only check who you are once at the login screen. Instead, it may continue to check your identity quietly while you use the device. This could include biometrics such as face recognition, fingerprint scanning, voice patterns, and maybe even behavior-based signals like how you type, move the mouse, hold your phone, or walk.

I do not think biometrics alone should replace everything. They are convenient, but they also create privacy risks. If a password is stolen, you can change it. If biometric data is stolen, you cannot change your face or fingerprint. That is why future authentication should combine biometrics with device security, encryption, and user control.

By 2040, the best authentication system should be secure but also simple for normal users. People should not feel like security is a constant obstacle. At the same time, they should still have the right to know what data is collected and how it is protected.

So, I believe the future is not just "passwordless". It is smarter, more invisible, and more personal authentication but only if privacy and choice are taken seriously.

AI in Academia

Legal or Illegal?

Three academic situations based on the traffic light model: one acceptable, one questionable, and one clearly inappropriate.

Read each scenario first. Then click “Show category” to reveal whether the AI use is green, yellow, or red.
Case 01 · AI in Academia

Using AI to create revision questions from personal lecture notes

A student attends class, takes notes, and studies the topic independently. After that, the student uses an AI tool to turn their own lecture notes into quiz questions for revision before the exam.

GREEN: Acceptable use that supports learning

This is acceptable because the student is still doing the learning and creating the original material. AI is only used as a revision helper and does not replace the student’s own work.

What green means: AI supports the learning process without replacing independent thinking or academic effort.

Case 02 · AI in Academia

Using AI to suggest the structure of a research paper

A student has already read the sources and written down ideas, but then asks AI to suggest a better structure for the final paper and possible section headings.

YELLOW: Use with caution

This can be helpful for organization, but it becomes questionable if the student lets AI shape the whole argument or relies on it too heavily. The final structure and academic reasoning should still be the student’s own.

What yellow means: AI may be useful, but it must not weaken the student’s own thinking, judgment, or academic responsibility.

Case 03 · AI in Academia

Submitting an AI-written discussion section in a lab report

A student uploads lab results to an AI tool, asks it to write the discussion and conclusion, changes only a few words, and submits the report without telling the instructor.

RED: Inappropriate academic use

This is inappropriate because the discussion and conclusion are a central part of the student’s own analysis. Submitting AI-written academic content as personal work is misleading and violates academic integrity.

What red means: AI replaces the student’s own academic work and crosses the boundary of acceptable use.
